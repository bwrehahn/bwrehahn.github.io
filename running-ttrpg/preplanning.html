Planning the Game

What You Will Need

Here are some of the things you will need when running an RPG. You will need:

  • Dice. These determine how well either Players' Characters or the Game Master's characters do at a task.
  • Pencils and Paper. Its in the name of the website. You will need these to keep notes and track of certain things throughout the game.
  • The Game Book. This has all the rules and can be used to as a reference if there is a dispute in the rules.
  • Character Sheets (can make copies from the back of the book or some companies have PDF files you can download).
  • Space to play. This will need to have limited distractions and needs to have enough room to accomodate all players around a table.
  • Optional: Game Screen. This is basically a flat piece of either cardboard or paper that has a summary of commonly referenced fules for a game. it looks something like this:

  • Optional: Cheat Sheets. This can be used in place or in addtion to the Game Screen. These are lists of commonly reference rules that you can make for yourself or the other players who might not have a copy of the book. They can also be lists of monsters or other charaters the Game Master created for the players to go up against.
  • Optional: Snacks. These games can go on for a while and having something to eat or drink can be nice.

Pre-Planning

In this section we will discuss Pre-planning. This is important because if you cannot get this information discussed in this section sorted and verified you might as well save yourself some time and not go any farther.

1. Do you want to play a game?

Role-playing isn't for everyone, and if you can't find anyone to play with, do not bother going any farther.

2. What do you want to play?

It is important to figure out what game you and your group of friends want to play. If they do want to play, suggest options. There are many types of Role-Playing games out there, such as: Science fiction, Fantasy, Horror, Super Hero, Steampunk, etc. You group's choice would be limited by the RPG books it has access to, but if needed, offer what books you can to the potential players so they can take a look and decide amongst themselves.

3. Decide when you are playing?

Remember to set a schedule. This could be once a day, week, every other weekend, etc. Everybody has different schedules to please make sure everyone can agree on a time that you can all get together regularly. Nobody wants to be left out of the action. If you can't arrange the scheduling, then your group is doomed before you start.

4. Where to play?

Decide where you want to play is also important. Like the time, it is important because you want to play in a place you all have easy access to. You can also have multiple locations if needed, such as going to one person's living room one week and then the next week going to another player's home basement if the first place is occupied that week. It is also important to find a place with few distractions. Nothing ruins a game more than outside distractions.

5. Rules Overview

If you have not read the RPG book for the game you are playing, you need to do so, there is no getting around that. If you have read the book, go ahead and review the rules. It is a good practice to be familiar with the rules. You do not have to memorize all the rules, that is impossible. But the more familiar you are with the rules, the less you will have to refer to those rules and the smoother the game will go. It is also helpful to be familiar with the book so you know where to look for the rules in the book in case you do need to reference them later. This is also where Game Screens and Cheat Sheets you make come in handy as mentioned previously.

Once you have all of these down you can begin planning the actual game.

Planning

Next is time to plan. This can both the easiest and hardest part, based on your own creativity.

1. Idea

This is the part that you figure out what you want the Players to achieve, the goals, missions, quest, world, rivals, and obstacles you want the characters to face. Inspirations can be anywhere, but here are a few of the most common sources:

  1. Books - Books can be a good source of ideas. For example, if you are playing a game based on the Wild West, you can read about how bandits had hideouts and booby-traps and can incorporate that into the game. Many game books have a starter adventure for people new to the game.
  2. Movies - Like books, Movies can be ripe with information. Say you are playing a science-fiction game and you are watching a movie with a villain you really like because they have a robotic arm. You can use that idea and put a character with a robotic arm in the game.
  3. Other people - It is a good practice to ask the Players if there is anything they want to do in the game. They can have good ideas and they really like it if you use their good ideas in the game. For example, if you are playing a fantasy game and a player wants to stop a wizard from burning down an orphanage, go ahead and use that.
  4. Yourself - You are full of ideas and may or may not know it. You can also use your ideas and combine them with the other sources.

When you create a story or mission for the game, make sure your ideas fit with the type of game you are playing. If you are playing a Horror game where the group has to defeat a werewolf and everybody is fighting for their own survival and nerves are tense, keep ideas that fit the horror theme of the game. Such as have them running in a dark hallway during a fool moon in a narrow hallway, fitting the theme you could have them find an old jewelry box with silver they can use to defeat the werewolf, but you shouldn't have them find a time traveling cowboy with laser pistols riding a dinosaur looking to beat up ninjas. The second option doesn't really fit with horror theme, no matter how much your friends might laugh at it.

Once you have the initial planning done, the next step is to prepare those idea and get ready for the game.

Preparation

This is the part where you make the actual preparations for the game. This is where you take those ideas you came up with in the planning phase polish them up and add any final touches you want, add any clues you want for the Players to find, plot twists and other unexpected surprises you want the Players to face and make them so you can use them in the game. In the Planning phase, they were just thoughts in your head, in this phase, they will become something you can put on paper and use to interact with your Players Characters they will be creating in the next phase.

  1. Overall Story - This is the overall sequence of events and the order you want them to happen in your game. For example, The story could be the Players are asked to meet a priest before they go into the haunted cemetery and he will be giving them a key to a mausoleum they need to get to. Once the Players do that they can get into the mausoleum and find a tunnel where witches are performing magic, but you want a twist that the witches are not really the cause of the haunting but the priest is actually a demon in disguise and the witches tried to stop him but he sealed them up in the tunnel and cannot get out. The story should have room for flexibility, because the players may do things you do not expect them to, hence learning as many rules as you can comes in handy from the previous step.
  2. Create NPC's - This is where you create the people the Players will be interacting with. Continuing the example above, you would be creating the Priest, the witches, the and the demon. This would involve creating characters and applying statistics and abilities. Player characters and Non-player characters all have these and you need to at least have something for all the NPC's. You need to do this for all NPC's because there is no telling what the Players will do. Continuing from the example, you may expect the Players to believe the witches story, but they may decide to not believe the witches and trust the priest and kill the witches just to be safe, and then they will have to fight, and although each game would have different rules for killing bad guys, they all use the character statistics and abilities to determine the outcome of the fight, and you need to be prepared for this and not have to go back to the book in case this happens.
  3. Create some standard NPC's that can be put into any situation. If this was a movie instead of a game, these characters would be extras walking around in the background. For example, you can create an NPC called "Normal person" with very basic statistic scores, and probably no special abilities like a witch from the previous example would have. This way, when one of you Players needs to interact with a normal person, such as a gas station attendant and ask for directions, you can use this NPC, and then you can use it again when another Player goes into the local McDonald's and need to order food. This method is also useful when creating generic monsters the Players will have to fight multiple times. Zombies are a good example. You can create the statics for 1 NPC and call it "Zombie" and you can duplicate and use the same NPC information for as many enemies as you need.
  4. Plan for Flexibility - As mentioned in Step 1, you need to plan on the Players not doing what you expect them to do. You have the example with the players not believe the witches above. They may also decide to skip speaking to the priest too and go directly to the mausoleum. In this case you can either just leave the priest out of the story and have the mausoleum unlocked, or you can have it locked and have them go back to the priest. They may also somehow find out the priest is a demon before even going into the mausoleum and decide to fight him right there and completely skip the witches. Do not force them to do something they do not want to do! Nothing brings a game to a grinding halt faster than the Game Masters and the Player arguing!
  5. Do not plan to kill the Players! - Remember, there is no winner or loser, everyone is here to create a story and play a game and have fun. Provide a challenge for them, but if they all die then it is game over and nobody likes that. It may be difficult to gauge how challenging you need to be so start easy and then if they need more challenging goals, give it to them and if they start struggling, tone it down a little. Remember, you as the Game Master control the world the Players are playing in.
  6. Plan to bring extras stuff - PPlayers can be forgetful so you may need to bring some extra items with you to keep the game going smoothly. Common item that are needed are pencils and paper, dice, character sheets (more on this in the next section) cheat sheets. You don't have to bring them yourself because they can be expensive, but make arrangements to make sure they are there.
  7. Snacks - As mention before this is optional, but playing a game can take a while and you want to have soda and snacks ready. You don't have to bring them yourself because they can be expensive, but you can have the other players either chip in and pay for part of them or have them bring the snacks themselves. After all, you are doing all this extra planning and preparation as the Game Master.
  8. Make any final changes - This is the point of no return. This is where you make sure you have everything ready to play. All NPC's made, all plot twists planned, all characters ready to go, any contingencies you can think of can be accounted for.

After this step you ready to host the game. You just need to make sure your Players are ready. That is the first part of Playing the Game.
